Connectedness

"You have faith in the links among all things. You believe there are few coincidences and that almost every event has meaning."

🧭
Section 01
What Is Connectedness

Official definition: "You have faith in the links among all things. You believe there are few coincidences and that almost every event has meaning."

Connectedness sits in the Relationship Building domain. About 12% of people who take CliftonStrengths have it in their top 5 (ranked ~22nd out of 34 themes). It is not common, which means most people around someone with high Connectedness will not share this talent.

Core mechanism: People with Connectedness perceive the world as a giant interconnected system. The nature of that system varies by individual — it can be spiritual or religious, scientific (cause and effect, physics), economic (everything is driven by incentives), ethical (a value set that links all behavior), or purely practical (turning off a light reduces the electricity bill). What is constant is the awareness of responsibility within that system: every action has a ripple effect, and nothing happens in isolation.

This is not about being "well-connected" in the networking sense. Connectedness is about perceiving, sensing, feeling, and anticipating links — between people, between ideas, between events, between time periods — that others miss.

Three conceptual "packages" Connectedness belongs to:

  • Values package (with Belief and Responsibility): Connectedness at its core values humanity in the broadest, most global sense.
  • Time package (with Context, Adaptability, Futuristic): Context is about the past; Adaptability lives in the present; Futuristic looks ahead; Connectedness is beyond time — it links past, present, and future simultaneously, and perceives meaning in the thread connecting them.
  • Collaboration package (with Harmony, Arranger, Includer, Consistency): Connectedness sees "us," not "us versus them." It perceives the whole.

Distinction from historically related theme: Belief

Early in the development of StrengthsFinder, Connectedness and Belief were combined as "Spirituality" because they overlapped. They were separated because neither requires a faith dimension. The difference: Belief is the rocks you stand on — firm core values that direct action. Connectedness is the understanding of where those rocks came from and the comfort in not needing those values to be rigidly defined.

Key distinctions from similar-sounding themes:

ThemeCore mechanismContrast with Connectedness
EmpathyFeels others' emotionsConnectedness senses what others contribute to the whole, not just what they feel
IdeationCreates new links between disparate phenomenaConnectedness perceives existing links; it is more contemplative and people-focused; Ideation creates on top of what Connectedness notices
HarmonyFinds common ground to resolve conflict and move forwardConnectedness sees conflict as leading to something meaningful — it gives conflict its due
AdaptabilityResponds to the present moment rather than controlling itConnectedness relates to forces outside of control by connecting to something bigger
AnalyticalNeeds proof and hard factsConnectedness accepts that some answers cannot be figured out — mystery itself is informative

Section 02
How It Shows Up

Perceptual signature: People with Connectedness describe it as seeing an overlay of lines connecting people, cultures, ideas, and events. Like rewatching a complex movie — they are catching all the clues the first time, while others miss them.

Behavioral patterns:

  • Listens at a deeper level — hears what is not being said, picks up on emotional evidence others overlook.
  • Can say with confidence: "I don't know how I know, but I just know."
  • Rarely needs to understand why something happened — the why-question does not help them figure things out.
  • Perceives people's roles and timelines as a web with many links, not as single linear trails.
  • Tracks results holistically — notices not just whether the plan was executed, but what actually happened, including deviations, and trusts both as part of something that needed to occur.
  • Experiences moments of sudden clarity in the middle of chaos: "Oh, it all makes sense now."
  • Takes action often through others — making sense by noticing what others can't, catching small actions that add up, connecting people who don't know they need to meet.
  • Is not easily destabilized by uncertainty — the unknown is simply part of a bigger picture, not a threat.
  • Often drawn to nature; finds stillness and connection in natural environments.

Descriptor words: acute observer, global thinker, intuitive, independent, patient, calm, wise, bridge-builder, integrator, humanitarian.

The calmness associated with Connectedness comes specifically from the belief that whatever is happening in the moment is only part of the moment — it leads to something bigger, whether in the past, future, or beyond current comprehension.

Section 03
Value for Team and Organization

Stabilizing force in chaos: When others are destabilized by uncertainty, someone with Connectedness can hold steady — not by having answers, but by trusting that the situation is part of something larger. This brings a qualitatively different kind of calm to teams under pressure.

Pattern recognition at organizational scale: In a board meeting, they can hear customer data and immediately connect it to a field observation from two months ago. They see relationships between phenomena that others would find completely unrelated. This is distinct from Ideation's generativity — Connectedness is grounding, not generative.

Bridge-builder across silos: Ideas, departments, and people in organizations often live in silos. Connectedness perceives the connections between them and can build bridges — toward shared mission, toward collaboration, toward innovation that emerges at the intersection.

Purpose-finding: People with Connectedness are naturally oriented toward purpose. In coaching or leadership, they can help teams and organizations articulate what they are actually trying to do in the world — often before the team can say it themselves.

Four follower needs Connectedness can serve (from leadership research):

  • Trust — by individualizing reassurance; understanding how each person best receives perspective (facts, one-on-one time, hands-on experience).
  • Stability — by expanding horizons beyond present chaos; helping people keep focus on the longer arc rather than the immediate crisis.
  • Compassion — by learning people's individual stories and what brought them to where they are; feeding genuine curiosity about the human experience behind the role.
  • Hope — by connecting today's struggle to a better tomorrow; asking questions that help people see how current difficulty leads somewhere.

    • Team magnet effect: Teams with someone high in Connectedness are often described as places others want to be. The talent is "always switched on" — even when it's not voiced, it shapes the tone and meaning of interactions.

    Section 04
    Dark Side / Risks

    Raw (undeveloped) Connectedness vs. mature Connectedness:

    RawMature
    A gut feeling left unexplainedGut feeling translated into shared insight or actionable direction
    "Something's not right" with no further articulation"Something's not right, and here's what the links tell us about where we're headed"
    Over-connecting — seeing connections that don't need to be thereBuilding bridges between connections that genuinely need to exist
    Trying to sell every connection to everyone, creating confusionAccepting that others won't always see what you see, and that's okay
    Appearing aloof or unaffected by immediate issuesBeing patient while still communicating care for the present

    Specific risks:

    • Acceptance as a default: Connectedness naturally leads with acceptance. This can mean not pushing the status quo when it genuinely needs to be pushed. The corrective is to get clear on what values are unchanging — what the person stands for even when the universe spins them out of control.
    • Perceived as naïve or not caring: Others who need evidence and direct answers may read Connectedness's comfort with mystery as indifference or lack of rigor. Without translation, the insights remain invisible.
    • Misidentified as spiritual: The theme frequently gets a spiritual/mystical reading, which can cause people with secular or analytical worldviews to reject it or feel the description doesn't fit — even when they clearly have the talent. The actual defining feature is systems perception, not spirituality.
    • Conspiracy pattern risk: In extreme expression, the impulse to find meaning in every event can drift into finding connections where they don't exist — pattern-matching without discrimination.
    • No built-in filter: Connectedness picks up on many signals simultaneously. Without other themes providing direction (Strategic, Deliberative, Achiever), it may generate too many connections without prioritizing which ones are worth acting on or voicing.

    Section 05
    How to Develop It

    Identify your tuning mechanism. Pay attention to when you feel most "tuned in" — your signal is strongest. Is it through study? Meditation? Physical activity? Time in nature? Specific relationships? Ask yourself: When do I feel most certain? When does my sense of something get loudest? Then build that into a deliberate practice.

    Clarify your anchor. Connectedness needs a personal or organizational value system to anchor it — otherwise acceptance becomes passivity. Ask: What is unchanging for me even when everything spins out of control? What connects everything for me — economics, ethics, spirituality, cause-and-effect? Without this clarity, the theme lacks direction.

    Build authentic relationships deliberately. Connectedness is a Relationship Building theme — proximity strengthens its signal. The closer you get to the people around you, the better you can perceive cause and effect, meaning, and direction. Authentic relationships are the aluminum foil on the radio receiver: they amplify the theme.

    Develop your translation skills. The biggest gap for most people with Connectedness is moving from internal perception to shared language. Practice:

    • Asking shorter, more open-ended questions (avoid stacking 5–6 questions into one)
    • Finding specific examples that illuminate a connection concretely
    • Using analogies and metaphors to make abstract links tangible
    • Speaking in the language of your audience — those who need facts need facts; those who need emotional resonance need that

    Learn to filter. Not every connection needs to be shared in every meeting. Strategic filtering — asking "is this connection strategically significant right now?" — is what makes the theme productive rather than overwhelming.

    As a leader: Show up in hard places. Ask questions that expand thinking. Illuminate what you notice. Anchor your perspective to what your followers need (trust, stability, compassion, hope). You don't need to have answers — Connectedness is at its best when it asks great questions, not when it provides declarations.

    Section 06
    How to Work With Someone Who Has Connectedness

    For managers:

    • Give them a runway. Don't demand immediate, linear answers. They may need to arrive at clarity organically — give them space to explore the connections before expecting a concrete recommendation.
    • Ask what they're noticing. The most powerful question for someone with Connectedness: "What are you noticing about our team / situation / project?" Invite them to share what they see. Treat them as a counselor on their own insight.
    • Check in about milestones. Help them align intermediate milestones to the ultimate goal. They track results holistically and may question whether every planned milestone is still serving the deeper purpose — that's useful friction worth engaging.
    • Explore their energy connections. Ask: "Where does your strongest sense of connection to this work come from?" Help them find the link between their personal purpose and the organization's mission — even if the line isn't straight.
    • Questions that unlock them: "What experience does this remind you of?" (past) / "What are you sensing right now?" (present) / "What do you think this will mean for us in six months?" (future).

    For colleagues:

    • Be present. Connect at a relationship level first — share something that matters to you, or ask how they're doing. Transactional interactions do not reach them.
    • Don't demand proof for their intuitions. If they say "I don't know why, but something feels off here" — investigate rather than dismiss. They are often picking up on something real that hasn't surfaced in the data yet.
    • Know your own ambiguity tolerance. People with Connectedness can sit with complexity. If you need a concrete answer or update, say so directly — they can calibrate. They can say "I don't have the answer" if they know that's what you need to hear.
    • Don't mistake patience for passivity. If they seem unconcerned during immediate chaos, they may be processing at a different level. Ask them what they see.
    • Partner for execution. They may need a partner who can take the connections they perceive and translate them into actionable plans or concrete communications.

    Section 07
    Combinations With Other Talents

    Connectedness + Empathy (most statistically common pairing): "I know we're here for a reason, and I feel what your part is in that reason." Empathy provides the emotional sensing of individuals; Connectedness provides the systemic understanding of why they're here and what they contribute to the whole. Together they create a deeply humanizing presence.

    Connectedness + Communication: Communication gives voice to what Connectedness senses but may struggle to articulate. Communication can translate systemic insights into stories and language others can receive. A highly productive pairing for coaching, facilitation, and organizational alignment.

    Connectedness + Deliberative (where Deliberative = careful, risk-aware decision-making): Deliberative checks for pitfalls; Connectedness checks for purpose and global impact. Together they create decisions that are both cautious and meaningful — "are we doing the right thing, and does it connect to something larger?"

    Connectedness + Discipline (Discipline = structure, predictability, organization): Discipline creates reliable structures through which Connectedness can express itself in a way others can follow. Without some structuring force, Connectedness's insights can feel unpredictable and hard to apply.

    Connectedness + Ideation: Ideation creates; Connectedness perceives. Ideation uses connections as a launching pad for something new; Connectedness uses them for depth and presence. Together they can identify both new possibilities and the relational/systemic context those possibilities exist within.

    Connectedness + Strategic (Strategic = pattern recognition for future planning): Strategic filters which connections are worth pursuing and navigates toward an outcome. Connectedness provides purpose and broader awareness. A person who uses Strategic to decide which connections from Connectedness are most relevant — and filters the rest — is operating the combination at its most effective.

    Connectedness + Achiever: Achiever ensures things actually get done; Connectedness ensures they get done for a reason. Without Achiever, Connectedness may stay in contemplation mode. Without Connectedness, Achiever may accomplish things efficiently but without deeper meaning.

    Connectedness + Context (Context = learns from the past to understand the present): Together they cover the full timeline — Context provides historical grounding, Connectedness provides the thread connecting all time periods. They naturally reinforce each other.

    Unlikely pairings (statistically rare, ~1% co-occurrence each): Competition, Command, Focus, Significance, Self-Assurance. These are largely self-determinant themes — they orient the person toward individual achievement, personal authority, or singular direction. Connectedness is outward- and systems-oriented, which is a different energy signature. When these combinations do occur, the two forces can productively tension-balance each other, but they require conscious integration.

    Connectedness + Adaptability: Both involve "letting go." Connectedness: there are forces outside my control; connecting to something bigger helps me navigate what I can control. Adaptability: I'm stronger responding to the present than trying to control it. Together they create extreme fluidity — high tolerance for uncertainty, very low need to impose structure. The risk: very little in the way of initiative or goal-setting without other themes providing direction.
