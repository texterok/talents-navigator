Includer

🧭
Section 01
What Is Includer

Official short definition: People exceptionally talented in Includer accept others. They show awareness of those who feel left out and make an effort to include them.

Core mechanic: Includer is a social-awareness talent. People with this theme carry a persistent, 360-degree radar for exclusion — at the level of individual people, demographic groups, or entire populations. The drive is not merely to notice exclusion but to act on it: facilitate involvement, expand the circle, make people feel like insiders.

"Includer believes everyone is an insider." [S2]

Includer belongs to the Relationship Building domain. Its excellence comes through connection to other people, not from achieving results independently.

Frequency: approximately 11–12% of people have Includer in their top 5 themes.

Key conceptual contrast with Maximizer (using strengths to achieve excellence): Maximizer has a discriminating, selective quality — sorting the best from the rest. Includer and Maximizer are often negatively correlated in profiles: when one is high, the other tends to be low.

Includer as a non-dominant theme: When Includer sits in positions 11–15, it can act as a filter on other talents — for example, causing someone high in Communication and Strategic to instinctively craft messages that land across diverse audiences, regardless of origin or viewpoint. [S2]

Section 02
How It Shows Up

Behavioral patterns

  • Notices absences. In any meeting, event, or group, the first question is often: who isn't here, and why?
  • Low threshold for invitation. It takes very little for an Includer to extend an invitation — "Hey, you can sit here" or "Come see what our team is working on" requires almost no deliberation.
  • Shops ideas before committing. Before committing to a solution, tends to run ideas by others — not for buy-in's sake, but because thinking through other people's perspectives is part of the cognitive process.
  • Sees commonalities before differences. The default lens is: what do we share? This makes it easy to feel comfortable among many different kinds of people quickly.
  • Inclusiveness conduit. Other people will relate to each other through the Includer. They act as the social node that makes introductions, bridges groups, and maintains connections that others wouldn't maintain on their own.
  • Two modes: Includer can operate publicly (advocating from the front of the room for excluded groups) or quietly (creating one-on-one pockets of comfort — a hand on the shoulder, a quiet invitation).

Section 03
Value for Team and Organization

Direct team contributions

  • Creates a welcoming environment. There is always a seat at the table when an Includer is on the team. New team members, peripheral contributors, and edge-of-group people integrate faster.
  • Cultivates innovation. By expanding who gets invited into conversations, Includer brings in perspectives and experiences that keep a team fresh, creative, and relevant.
  • Champion for the voiceless. Aware of sweeping generalizations that don't account for everyone; willing to stand up for individuals and groups who need representation.
  • Collaborative bridge across teams. An Includer can be assigned to partner intentionally with teams outside their own, enabling ideas and information to flow in both directions.
  • Recognition system. Includers notice who deserves recognition and are natural at distributing it across the group — not just to the most visible. Recognition, when it becomes contagious on a team, accelerates productivity.

Against the 5 Truths of Strong Teams (Gallup, Strengths Based Leadership)

  • Results over conflict: Includer naturally spots where people are coming from — especially those outside the team's gravitational center — and makes room for those differences without letting them become friction.
  • Organization over self: Includer expands focus from "our team" to "what are other teams doing, what does the whole organization need?" Feeding this talent with cross-functional partnership accelerates it.
  • Personal life matters: No social walls — just speed bumps. A large acquaintance network and natural ease with strangers makes the personal environment feel socially safe.
  • Embrace diversity: Broader circle → broader experience pool → higher baseline creativity and relevance.
  • Magnet for talent: The gift is social comfort — you feel like you fit in around an Includer. And the people who might otherwise never have been in your circle also feel like they fit in. That is structurally attractive to talent.

    Section 04
    Dark Side / Risks

    Blind spots the Includer needs to manage

    Adding unnecessary complexity. Including more people is obvious and effortless to Includer; it is not obvious to others that inclusion is worth the coordination cost. Adding voices to a decision process can slow it down, dilute accountability, or overwhelm timelines.

    Indiscriminate expansion. Raw Includer can invite so broadly that each individual invitation feels meaningless — "I was one of 50 people; this wasn't special." The cure: communicate why this specific person was invited.

    Over-soliciting input without closing. Gathering input from many people can lead to a situation where contributors expect their input to be incorporated. When it can't all be used, those contributors feel dismissed — sometimes worse than if they hadn't been consulted at all.

    Inward focus on personal exclusion. The same radar that detects others' exclusion can, in an underdeveloped state, fixate on one's own sense of being left out. This reads as needy or hypersensitive to colleagues who don't share the theme.

    Conflicting with efficiency-driven themes. Includer naturally wants to broaden; Focus (attention on priority), Achiever (drive to complete), and Maximizer (doing more of what already works best) pull toward narrowing. Without conscious management, this tension creates internal friction.

    Section 05
    How to Develop It

    These apply both to someone deepening their own Includer and to coaches helping others strengthen it.

    Make invitations a habit — not events. Keep the metaphorical door open permanently. People find stability in knowing they always have a seat at your table, even if they don't sit down every time. Reach back to people who haven't heard from you in a while.

    Practice articulating commonalities. The core strength is the ability to see what unites people. Get deliberate about naming it — out loud, in meetings, in writing. "Here's what we all have in common" is the Includer's most transferable contribution.

    Distinguish presence from representation. Not every inclusion act requires inviting someone into the room. A more sustainable practice: challenge people who are present to consider the perspectives of those who aren't. Act as a spokesperson — "I think we'd benefit from hearing X's view before we decide."

    Develop the output, not just the awareness. Raw Includer = noticing wrongdoing/exclusion but not acting. Mature Includer = translating awareness into a specific action: briefing someone afterward, reorganizing a meeting guest list, or explicitly advocating for an absent group in a decision.

    Use recognition as a tool. In meetings, make it a habit to call out something specific you appreciate about each person — in front of others. Recognition delivered publicly becomes contagious and positions the Includer as the connector through whom others relate to each other.

    Identify when to shrink the gap, not add to the room. Coach yourself: what is the best way to make this person feel part of something, even without direct involvement? Brief them. Mention their contribution. Reference their perspective. These are high-ROI inclusion moves that don't add coordination overhead.

    Section 06
    How to Work with Someone Who Has Includer

    For managers

    • Assign them to onboarding and integration. First-impression moments — new hires, cross-team meetings, client introductions — are natural Includer territory.
    • Give them the "who's missing?" role. Before finalizing any guest list, decision body, or stakeholder map, ask them to review it. They will spot the gaps.
    • Ask "who else should we invite?" at the end of planning meetings. This question honors the talent and consistently surfaces overlooked voices.
    • Don't use exclusivity as a motivator. VIP access, small-group privileges, exclusive offers — these don't appeal to Includer and can actively demotivate. Frame incentives around togetherness and group benefit.
    • Talk about the experience of the whole, not singling out individuals or groups.
    • Feed the talent with cross-functional scope. Asking them to partner more intentionally with outside teams gives them the expanded circle they naturally want to operate within.

    For colleagues

    • Understand you're communicating with them as part of a whole. When talking with an Includer, be prepared for other people to have a presence in their thinking. They may naturally route the conversation toward group impact.
    • Ask for their read on inclusion. "Do you think we've accounted for everyone who should have a voice in this?" is a genuinely useful question and honors what they see naturally.
    • Don't be surprised by broad guest lists. It's not disorganization; it's a feature. The question to ask them is: "Given our timeline, which of these people are critical to have present now vs. briefed afterward?"
    • Recognize that their social comfort is a gift to you. Their presence in a group lowers the social temperature and encourages authenticity. This is a real productivity enabler, even if it's hard to measure.

    Section 07
    Combinations with Other Talents

    Most likely statistical pair: Includer + Positivity (≈38% co-occurrence)

    Both themes are others-focused and driven by awareness of how other people feel. Positivity notices how people feel and tries to improve it; Includer notices whether people feel part of the group and tries to close that gap.

    • Together: create an extremely warm, energizing social environment. May be mistaken for Woo (winning others over) because the combined energy is similar — expansive, upbeat, welcoming.
    • Separately: Includer without Positivity is still socially aware and active, but quieter, less energetic — the person who knows every name in the organization and something meaningful about each one. Positivity without Includer may focus on individuals rather than group cohesion.

    Least likely pair: Includer + Command (occurs ≈1% of the time)

    Command (creating clarity, taking charge, confronting directly) and Includer pull in different directions temperamentally — Command towards black-and-white, forward momentum; Includer towards tolerance of ambiguity and patience with diverse inputs.

    However: Command + Includer together can produce a social justice archetype — someone who sees exclusion clearly, names it publicly, and demands action. [S2]

    High-value complementary pairs

    Includer + Individualization (recognizing what makes each person unique): Individualization is drawn to differences and treats people differently because they are different. Includer is drawn to commonalities. Together, they form a complete picture: what unites us AND what makes each contribution distinct. A partner with Individualization can help an Includer not just bring people in, but communicate why each specific person is uniquely valuable to a situation.

    Includer + Strategic (finding the best route forward among alternatives): Includer with a Strategic lens becomes skilled at mapping who the right people are, what role they should play, and what should happen with those who aren't directly involved. This produces intentional, defensible inclusion rather than reflexive expansion.

    Includer + Arranger (orchestrating people and resources for maximum productivity): Similar to Strategic — helps Includer translate "who should be here" into "who does what, in what configuration." Arranger adds the operational structure that prevents inclusion from becoming chaos.

    Includer + Focus (priority and follow-through): Focus provides the boundary that Includer's natural expansiveness needs. High acceptance of people (Includer) + high expectations for outcomes (Focus) is described as a particularly powerful combination — people feel genuinely welcomed and genuinely held accountable. [S1]

    Includer + Harmony (avoiding conflict, seeking consensus): Together, these help process large amounts of diverse input and find workable consensus quickly — useful for facilitation-heavy roles.

    Includer + Connectedness (belief in the universal connectedness of all things): Both themes have a sense of openness and beyond-the-individual orientation. Connectedness adds a philosophical foundation for why inclusion matters; Includer drives the behavioral execution.
