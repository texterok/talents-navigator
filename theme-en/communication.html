Section 01
What is Communication

Official short definition: People exceptionally talented in Communication generally find it easy to put their thoughts into words. They are good conversationalists and good presenters.

Deeper definition: Communication is a passionate, natural relationship with words — spoken or written — that produces both conceptual clarity and social connection through verbal expression. At its core, it is a drive to be understood and to help others be understood. It belongs to the Influencing domain, though it operates across both influencing and relationship-building directions depending on the person's other themes.

Communication occurs in approximately 13% of people's top-5 profiles — a middle-range frequency.

What it is not:

  • Not just talking. It includes listening, dialogue, and the ability to give words to what others are struggling to say.
  • Not manipulation. Messaging and crafting language is distinct from deception.
  • Not an executing theme. Communication affects how people feel and think; it does not inherently drive task completion.

Distinctions from similar themes:

  • vs. Command (takes charge, confronts): Command uses direct clarity of decisions to direct attention — "Here's what we need to do." Communication uses ease with words to draw people in — "I think what we're all saying is..."
  • vs. Woo (winning others over): Woo makes people feel seen through charm and connection. Communication makes people feel heard through articulation. Woo opens the door; Communication furnishes the room with meaning.

Section 02
How It Shows Up

Processing through words. People with high Communication find clarity by talking or writing things out when confused or frustrated. Ideas in their head exist in a language they don't fully understand until spoken or written. Verbalization is their translation mechanism, not their performance.

Articulating what others can't. They can compute quickly from a half-formed thought to precise words — both their own and other people's. Hearing "that's exactly what I meant, I just didn't have the words" is a common experience.

Story orientation. They are drawn to storytelling as a structure for meaning — both creating stories and collecting them. They tend to debrief events, explain relationships, and frame problems through narrative.

Audience awareness. They read body language and group reactions continuously — in one-on-one conversation and in rooms of hundreds. They are tracking whether the message is landing, not just being delivered.

Mining for gems. Within a paragraph others hear as flat, someone with high Communication spots the "shinier" phrases — license-plate statements, emotionally resonant words — that can crystallize an idea and make it stick.

Drive to be understood. Being misunderstood produces a distinct discomfort that cannot be dropped. They will try again to clarify, often finding a different angle or a better example until the message connects.

Language mirroring. They pick up quickly on the lingo, acronyms, and cultural vocabulary of clients or new environments and naturally weave it back into their communication. This creates fast trust and a sense of "they get us."

Personal signature. Outside work: signature jokes, habitual rapport-builders, asking permission before telling stories ("Can I tell you a quick story?"), debriefing their day to others through narrative.

Section 03
Value for Team and Organization

Message translation. Translates complicated or abstract ideas into digestible language that different people can receive. Like great teachers, they find multiple ways to say the same thing until it lands.

Attention direction. Can shift where a group is focusing — away from conflict and toward results, away from noise and toward a key message. Particularly valuable in chaos or change: they can summarize clearly, provide context, and hold the group to a through-line.

Progress measured through adoption. Tracks how much buy-in, how many people educated, how widely an idea has spread. Progress for Communication is social and relational, not just task-based.

Internal public relations. Knows what to say, when to say it, and who to say it with (not just to). Can package the team's message for different audiences — internal stakeholders, customers, leadership — without losing the core.

Champion of others' ideas. Can advocate for someone else's idea with the same skill and energy as their own. Will amplify a message that deserves more airtime, or surface a perspective that wasn't being heard.

Thought partnership. Helps others replay what they're trying to say — "let me reflect back what I'm hearing" — and determine whether it's actually what they mean once it's in words.

Trust through language. By speaking someone's language back to them, they create rapid rapport and credibility, particularly with new clients, audiences, or organizational cultures.

As a leader: Gives teams the words they need. Boils brainstormed ideas down into sticky, palatable phrases people can carry with them. Breathes life and emotion into dry concepts so they're never boring. Helps people see potential they can't yet feel by painting a vivid picture of what success looks like.

Section 04
Dark Side / Risks

Raw vs. mature: The same talent that produces clarity can produce noise. In undeveloped or low-energy form:

Raw formMature form
Chatty — everything in the brain exits through the mouthEnergetic — words are selected and weighted
Verbose — no filterPithy — exactly the right words
Long-winded — fills spaceFar-reaching — words have gravity
Loose-lipped — transparency without discretionSincere — transparent but controlled
Always on — performing for any available audienceAudience-aware — tailors expression to the specific people and moment

Eloquent rambling is still rambling. Success at speaking can produce the illusion of substance. People with high Communication sound good even when they're processing, which means others may treat unfinished thinking as a final position.

Unintentional commitments. Thinking out loud means occasionally saying things before fully meaning them. Others may hold them to words that were part of the process, not the conclusion.

Speed mismatch. Most people take longer to form and express thoughts than Communication does. Silence or slow responses are not disengagement — but someone with high Communication can read them that way and fill the gap unnecessarily.

Crowding out listening. High success at speaking can reduce investment in listening. World-class Communication requires equally world-class listening.

Not a natural equity lens. Communication does not inherently notice who isn't getting a voice in the room. That awareness typically comes from relationship-building themes (Empathy, Includer, Developer). Don't assume a team's Communication person is ensuring everyone is heard — they're optimizing for the message, not the inclusion.

Section 05
How to Develop It

Invest in listening explicitly. In meetings, write down a question rather than immediately jumping in with the observation. Practice letting others finish. Learn the three levels of listening: what is said, what is not verbalized (nonverbal cues), and what is being left out entirely.

Think out loud before it counts. Must rehearse by speaking, not reading. If a presentation, teaching session, or important conversation hasn't been said out loud beforehand, the first attempt will be the live one — and it will show. Find a safe practice partner whose role is to hear the thinking, not hold you to every word.

Develop version control for your messages. Practice the 90-second version and the 5-minute version of any key message. Know which one fits the current moment.

Make editing a habit. Write shorter emails — say what you want to say, then cut it. Apply the Coco Chanel principle: before you leave the house, take one thing off. What is the essence of this?

Capture insights in the moment. Some of the best thinking surfaces mid-conversation. Keep a notepad or voice recorder nearby. Ideas said aloud that aren't captured often disappear entirely.

Study the craft. Join Toastmasters, take a creative writing class, binge a show with a sophisticated plot and pay attention to how the plot is constructed and paced. Read — not just to consume but to study what makes certain language land.

Ask great questions. Well-researched, thoughtful questions are an extension of Communication, not a replacement for it. Questions that get to the heart of an answer produce more than observations stated pithily.

Section 06
How to Work with Someone Who Has Communication

For managers:

  • Give them an audience. Connect them to customers, clients, stakeholders, or followers. Help them understand who is receiving their message — this amplifies the talent dramatically.
  • Use them as a communication resource, not just a communicator. When you're struggling to get through to someone, or need to craft key messaging, ask them: "What questions should I be asking?" or "What do you think I'm really trying to say?"
  • Create dedicated communication time. They don't need a problem to have a conversation. A 30-minute regular exchange where they can process out loud is valuable in itself.
  • Don't mistake the thinking-out-loud phase for the final position. Ask: "Now that you've talked through this, what do you actually think?" This respects their process and ensures you're hearing the conclusion.

For colleagues:

  • When they appear to go back and forth on something, resist the urge to pin them down in the middle. Let them process. Follow up.
  • Forgive rambling. It's often how they arrive at something precise. Staying with them through the iteration pays off.
  • If they're unusually quiet in a meeting, they may be holding back deliberately. Check in rather than assuming passivity.
  • Don't hang on every word as though it's permanent. They may say things to find out if they mean them.
  • Pair them with people who struggle to be heard if you want those people to get more airtime — Communication won't do this automatically, but they're effective amplifiers when pointed in that direction.

For people who have low Communication:

  • Allow more preparation time before high-visibility moments.
  • Find a trusted reviewer for emails, presentations, and key messages before they go out.
  • Think about what story belongs behind the data — the numbers alone don't carry the message.

Section 07
Combinations with Other Talents

Natural pairing: Woo (Winning Others Over)

The most statistically common partner. Both themes are outward-focused and other-oriented. Together: they assemble a cast of characters (Woo opens doors, Communication builds the story), making personal connections through narrative. The combination produces charismatic, relational influencing.

Least likely pairing: Deliberative

Deliberative is about caution, risk assessment, and careful decision-making before acting. When combined with Communication, the result is a more careful communicator — someone who still needs to talk things through, but has a clearer internal signal for when the thinking is done and it's time to move. Rare, but not contradictory.

Complementary partnerships

Communication + Restorative (problem-solver): Communication brings attention and energy; Restorative brings focus on a specific problem. Together they're effective troubleshooters who can both diagnose and articulate the path forward.

Communication + Maximizer (best-from-good): Maximizer sorts for the highest-potential ideas; Communication generates many and provides the vehicle to socialize the best ones. Maximizer lifts the quality; Communication multiplies the reach.

Communication + Focus (clear direction): Focus brings goal-specificity and singular energy; Communication converts that individual drive into collective momentum. Together: not just "I know where I'm going" but "let's all go there together."

Communication + Harmony (consensus-seeking): Produces a calm, practical voice that can restate high-level concepts in accessible language, de-escalate charged conversations, and ensure the message lands without friction.

Communication + Intellection (deep reflection): Requires deliberate preparation — must think before speaking — but the output is unusually substantive and precise. Slower to express, but more considered.

Communication alone (without executing themes): Communication is not an executing theme — it does not naturally sort toward what needs to get done. Pairing with themes like Achiever, Restorative, Discipline, or Responsibility adds the action dimension that converts articulated ideas into completed work.
